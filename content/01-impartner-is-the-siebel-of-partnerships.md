# Why Impartner Is the Siebel of Partnerships

**A note to every Head of Partnerships still paying $80K a year to run a program from a 2015-era portal: the ground under your feet is moving.**

In 1999, Siebel was the biggest name in enterprise software. It had the CRM market locked up — a multi-year implementation, an army of consultants, a client list that read like the Fortune 500. Nobody was going to beat Siebel at Siebel's game.

Salesforce didn't try. They built something structurally different: cloud instead of on-premise, subscription instead of license, self-serve instead of consulting-heavy, one data model instead of ten. For a while, Siebel's customers laughed at the feature gap. Then they didn't. Then Siebel was gone.

Partner software is in its Siebel moment right now. Impartner, Salesforce PRM, and the rest of the incumbent stack are running the same playbook Siebel ran in 2001 — feature completeness, enterprise sales motion, consulting-led implementations, single-tenant thinking. It worked for twenty years. It's about to stop working, for three structural reasons none of them can fix without rebuilding from scratch.

---

## Reason 1: They're Built for One Vendor. Partners Work With Many.

Here's the thing every partner rep at a consulting SI already knows but no incumbent has internalized:

A partner doesn't have one program to manage. They have five, or ten, or twenty.

A mid-sized Snowflake SI partner might also resell Databricks, carry an AWS practice, refer Salesforce deals, and co-sell with three SaaS vendors. Each of those vendors runs their own "partner portal." Each portal has its own login, its own data model, its own commission structure, its own deal registration form. The partner logs into all of them. Or, realistically, logs into none of them and emails the vendor's partner manager instead.

Impartner and Salesforce PRM are built on a structural assumption: **the portal serves one vendor's program.** That architecture made sense in 2010. It's catastrophic in 2026, because the people who actually generate partner revenue — the reps at the partner organizations — don't live inside one vendor's walled garden. They live across the ecosystem.

The next generation of partner software inverts this. The partner gets *one* portal. The vendor gets *one* customer. The data flows both ways. The partner sees every vendor they work with in a single view, with AI that understands where to spend their next hour to maximize their pipeline across the network.

Once that exists, the single-vendor portal becomes the fax machine of partnerships. Not because it doesn't work — because nobody wants to use five of them.

Impartner cannot ship this without dismantling the core business model that funds them: each vendor paying $30K to $150K a year for their own instance. They won't do it. They literally can't, without killing the company first.

This is the defining structural vulnerability of every incumbent in this space, and it will be the wedge that ends them.

---

## Reason 2: They're Built for Admins. The Next Decade Belongs to Operators With AI Copilots.

Open Impartner. Open Salesforce PRM. Open any legacy partner portal. Tell me what you see.

You see a dashboard full of dashboards. You see a module for deal registration, a module for MDF, a module for training, a module for analytics, a module for onboarding, a module for co-marketing. You see a tree of settings pages. You see forms that ask for data you don't have and fields that don't match your program.

You do *not* see anyone doing their job.

These products are built for the administrator — the person configuring the portal, not the person using it. That made sense when enterprise software was sold top-down, implemented by consultants, and used reluctantly by the people paying for it. It makes no sense now, because every other category of software has moved on.

Sales tools now proactively draft emails, surface stalled deals, brief reps before calls. Legal tools generate first drafts. Finance tools reconcile automatically. Developer tools pair with engineers in real time. The operator of a modern software product does not navigate menus. They ask questions and confirm answers.

A VP of Partnerships who's running a 150-partner program in 2027 is not going to open seventeen dashboards on Monday morning. They're going to open one chat interface, ask *"what changed this weekend, what's at risk, and what do I need to do today,"* and get a real answer with citations, a recommendation, and a one-click accept.

No incumbent is building that. They're adding Einstein buttons to the same screens. Every AI feature they ship is a bolt-on, billed separately, implemented by the same consulting army that sold the original portal. You're paying 2015 prices for a 2015 product with a 2024 chatbot stuck to the side.

The winner here isn't whoever has the best AI feature. It's whoever rebuilds the product *around* the AI layer, where the conversational interface is the primary navigation and the old modules are reduced to surfaces the AI reads from and writes to.

---

## Reason 3: They're Priced for a World That No Longer Exists.

The partner software market has always charged per vendor, per seat, per year, with a six-figure implementation. That pricing structure assumes three things that are all breaking simultaneously:

1. **Partner programs are expensive, top-down investments.** In 2026, the fastest-growing programs are ecosystem-led and partner-pulled. The cost of standing up a program should be an afternoon, not a quarter.
2. **The buyer has budget.** In 2026, partnership teams are being asked to do more with less, not less with more. The AE pitching Impartner at $80K/year is fighting a purchase order that didn't exist last year and won't exist next year.
3. **Value is proven by features.** In 2026, value is proven by outcomes. The CFO doesn't want a list of modules. They want to know how much partner-sourced pipeline closed last quarter and what it cost to track.

The pricing model that wins the next decade is a platform fee the buyer can absorb without procurement, plus outcome-based upsells that only trigger when the product delivers real value, plus a share of the commission volume the platform actually processes. That last piece is the Gusto/Stripe model — we make money when you make money, not when you sign a contract.

This is another structural vulnerability Impartner cannot fix without breaking itself. Their sales team is compensated on six-figure ACVs. Their implementation partners are compensated on configuration hours. Their entire go-to-market depends on the contract being big and the onboarding being slow. You don't rebuild that. You wait for it to die.

---

## What Actually Replaces Them

The company that takes the partner software category in the next decade will look structurally like Day.ai looks to Salesforce, or Linear looks to Jira, or Figma looked to Sketch. Not a feature-for-feature copy with a cheaper sticker — a different product with a different shape.

Three things define it:

**One.** A partner portal that's the same product whether you're a 20-person SI working with five vendors or a 2,000-person global distributor. Multi-vendor by default. One login, one data model, one AI copilot that sees across the full ecosystem the partner actually operates in.

**Two.** AI as the primary interface, not a feature. The operator doesn't navigate to a module. They ask a question. The product answers, cites, recommends, and offers to act. The old modules still exist underneath — they're just not how you touch the product anymore. The AI is how you touch the product. Everything else is substrate.

**Three.** Pricing that reflects what the product actually does. Flat platform fee, compute absorbed by the vendor. Outcome-based pricing on high-value AI workflows. Commission volume share on the transactions the platform processes. No implementation fee. No per-seat gouging. No consulting partner extracting rent on configuration.

This is the shape of the category five years from now. The incumbents know this and cannot move. Their customers know this and are waiting for something real to switch to.
